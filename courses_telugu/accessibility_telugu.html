What is Web Accessibility

Web Accessibility means designing websites that everyone can use, including people with disabilities.

Some people may not see, hear, or move in the same way as others, so websites should be easy for them too.

For example, blind users use screen readers to hear the content of a page.

Web Accessibility includes adding text for images, using proper color contrast, and allowing keyboard navigation.

Making your website accessible helps more people use it and shows you care about equal access for all.

Why is Accessibility Important?

Accessibility is important because everyone should be able to use websites, apps, and online services.

People with disabilities may face problems if websites are not made in an accessible way.

When websites are accessible, it helps blind users, people with low vision, deaf users, and many others.

Accessible design makes the internet fair and usable for everyone, not just for people without disabilities.

It also improves the overall user experience and follows legal and ethical standards.

Benefits of Accessible Websites

Accessible websites offer many benefits for users and businesses.

When a website is accessible, more people can use it easily — including people who are blind, deaf, or have difficulty using a mouse or reading text.

Over 1 billion people in the world live with some kind of disability. By making your website accessible, you open your content to a much larger audience.

Businesses with accessible websites can attract more customers, improve customer satisfaction, and increase profits.

Search engines like Google also favor accessible websites, which helps your website appear higher in search results.

There are also legal reasons to make websites accessible. Many countries, like the United States, follow laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

If a website is not accessible, the business can face legal action, heavy fines, and damage to its reputation.

For example, if an online shopping site is not accessible, blind users may not be able to browse products or complete a purchase.

This means the business loses customers and money. On the other hand, an accessible shopping site can be used by everyone, including people using screen readers or keyboard navigation.

Accessible sites also load better, work on all devices, and give a better experience to all users — not just people with disabilities.

In short, making your website accessible is good for people, good for business, and good for the law.

Legal and Ethical Aspects of Accessibility

Making websites accessible is both a legal requirement and a moral responsibility in many parts of the world.

Many countries have specific laws that require websites to be accessible to people with disabilities.

These laws are in place to make sure everyone has equal access to digital content, services, and online tools.

If businesses or organizations do not follow these laws, they may face legal action or financial penalties.

Ethically, web accessibility is about fairness and giving everyone the same opportunity to use the internet.

Following accessibility guidelines, like the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines), helps create a better and more inclusive web for all.

Introduction to Accessibility Standards and Guidelines

Accessibility standards and guidelines help developers and designers make websites usable for everyone, including people with disabilities.

The most important and widely used guideline is called WCAG – Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

These guidelines are created by the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) to make web content easier to see, hear, and use.

Other important standards like ARIA and Section 508 also support accessibility by guiding how websites and apps should behave.

Following these standards ensures that websites are clear, simple to navigate, and work with assistive technologies like screen readers.

Types of Disabilities and Their Assistive Technologies

Visual Impairments

Visual impairment encompasses a range of conditions affecting eyesight, from low vision to complete blindness. It can be caused by various factors, including congenital conditions, diseases, and injuries. Understanding the different types of visual impairments is crucial for creating accessible web content.

Types of Visual Impairments:
  • Blindness: Complete or near-complete loss of vision. Users rely heavily on non-visual means to access information.
  • Low Vision: Reduced visual acuity that cannot be fully corrected with glasses or contact lenses. Users may benefit from screen magnification, high contrast, and alternative text sizes.
  • Color Blindness (Color Vision Deficiency): Difficulty or inability to distinguish certain colors. Design should not rely solely on color to convey important information.
Assistive Technologies for Visual Impairments:
  • Screen Readers: Software that reads aloud the text and describes non-textual elements (images, buttons, etc.) on a computer screen. Examples include JAWS, NVDA, and VoiceOver.
  • Screen Magnifiers: Software that enlarges a portion of the screen, making it easier for users with low vision to see content.
  • Braille Displays: Hardware devices that translate text into Braille, allowing blind users to read digital content.
  • Voice Recognition Software: Enables users to control their computer and input text using voice commands.
  • High Contrast Themes: Operating system or browser settings that increase the color contrast, improving readability for users with low vision.

Auditory Impairments

Auditory impairment refers to a partial or total inability to hear. This can range from mild hearing loss to profound deafness and can significantly impact how users interact with multimedia content on the web.

Types of Auditory Impairments:
  • Deafness: A profound loss of hearing, often requiring communication through sign language or visual aids.
  • Hard of Hearing: Mild to moderate hearing loss, where users may have difficulty hearing soft sounds or understanding speech, especially in noisy environments.
Assistive Technologies for Auditory Impairments:
  • Captions and Subtitles: Textual representations of the audio content in videos and audio recordings, synchronized with the media.
  • Transcripts: Text versions of audio content, providing access to information for users who cannot hear the audio.
  • Sign Language Interpretation (Video): Videos of sign language interpreters can be embedded alongside audio content.
  • Hearing Aids: Electronic devices that amplify sound for individuals with hearing loss. While not directly a web technology, accessible design considers their use.
  • Visual Alerts: Instead of or in addition to auditory cues, visual notifications can be used to alert users to events.

Motor Impairments

Motor impairments involve limitations in physical movement, coordination, or dexterity. These can be caused by conditions such as cerebral palsy, multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injuries, arthritis, or repetitive strain injuries. Accessible web design ensures that users with motor impairments can navigate and interact with content effectively.

Types of Motor Impairments:
  • Paralysis: Complete or partial loss of muscle function in one or more parts of the body.
  • Weakness: Reduced muscle strength, making it difficult to perform certain movements.
  • Tremors: Involuntary shaking movements that can affect fine motor control.
  • Lack of Coordination: Difficulty controlling and coordinating movements.
Assistive Technologies for Motor Impairments:
  • Keyboard Navigation: Allowing users to navigate and operate all interactive elements using only the keyboard (Tab key, Arrow keys, Enter key, Spacebar).
  • Switch Devices: Input devices that can be activated by a single movement (e.g., pressing a button, puffing air), often used with on-screen keyboards or scanning techniques.
  • Eye-Tracking Devices: Allow users to control the computer cursor and interact with the screen using their eye movements.
  • Head Pointers: Devices worn on the head that allow users to control the cursor by moving their head.
  • Voice Recognition Software: Enables hands-free control of the computer and text input.
  • On-Screen Keyboards: Virtual keyboards that can be operated with a mouse, trackball, switch device, or touch screen.

Cognitive Impairments

Cognitive impairments affect mental functions such as memory, attention, language processing, problem-solving, and comprehension. These can include learning disabilities (like dyslexia), attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), autism spectrum disorder, and memory loss due to aging or injury. Accessible web design aims to make content clear, organized, and easy to understand for users with cognitive differences.

Types of Cognitive Impairments:
  • Learning Disabilities (e.g., Dyslexia, Dysgraphia): Affect how individuals process and learn information, particularly related to reading, writing, and spelling.
  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): Characterized by difficulties with attention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD): A range of conditions characterized by challenges with social interaction, communication, and repetitive behaviors; sensory sensitivities can also be present.
  • Memory Impairments: Difficulties with recalling information, which can be caused by aging, brain injury, or conditions like dementia.
Assistive Technologies and Strategies for Cognitive Impairments:
  • Screen Readers (for text-to-speech): Can aid users with dyslexia or reading difficulties.
  • Text-to-Speech Tools: Software that reads selected text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Tools: Can help users who struggle with writing.
  • Simplified Language and Layout: Using clear, concise language and consistent, uncluttered layouts.
  • Visual Aids and Multimedia: Incorporating images, videos, and diagrams to support understanding.
  • Consistent Navigation: Providing clear and predictable navigation structures.
  • পর্যাপ্ত সময়সীমা (Adequate Time Limits): Allowing users enough time to read and complete tasks.
  • Error Prevention and Recovery: Providing clear instructions and easy ways to correct mistakes.

Overview of WCAG

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are internationally recognized standards developed by the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) to ensure that websites and digital content are accessible to people with disabilities. These guidelines help web developers, designers, and organizations create content that can be used by everyone, including people with visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, language, learning, and neurological disabilities.

What is WCAG?

WCAG stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. It is a set of recommendations for making web content more accessible, especially for individuals with disabilities. The goal is to ensure equal access and opportunity to people with different abilities. These guidelines are developed by the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) of the W3C. WCAG addresses a wide range of accessibility issues, from readable text and navigable interfaces to support for screen readers and keyboard users.

The Structure of WCAG

WCAG is structured around four guiding principles known as POUR:

  • Perceivable – Information and user interface components must be presented to users in ways they can perceive (e.g., providing text alternatives for images).
  • Operable – Interface components and navigation must be operable (e.g., all functionality must be accessible using a keyboard).
  • Understandable – Information and operation of the user interface must be understandable (e.g., clear instructions and predictable navigation).
  • Robust – Content must be robust enough to be interpreted reliably by assistive technologies, including screen readers and future technologies.

Each principle contains guidelines, which are further divided into success criteria. These criteria are testable and help determine whether the content meets accessibility requirements.

WCAG Versions

Over time, WCAG has evolved to address new technologies and user needs. The major versions include:

  • WCAG 1.0 – Released in 1999. This was the first version of the guidelines, focusing mainly on HTML and web page accessibility for screen reader users. It had 14 guidelines and was more technical in nature.
  • WCAG 2.0 – Released in 2008. This version introduced the POUR principles and was technology-neutral. It included 12 guidelines and 61 success criteria. It is still widely referenced in legal and policy frameworks.
  • WCAG 2.1 – Released in 2018. It built on WCAG 2.0 by adding 17 new success criteria, focusing on mobile accessibility, low vision users, and people with cognitive or learning disabilities.
  • WCAG 2.2 – Released in 2023. This version added 9 more success criteria to better support users with low vision, cognitive disabilities, and users on mobile and touch-based devices.
  • WCAG 3.0 (Draft) – Currently under development. It represents a major shift, aiming to be more flexible, user-focused, and able to address a wider range of disabilities and content types. It may include scoring mechanisms rather than simple pass/fail outcomes.

WCAG Conformance Levels

Each success criterion in WCAG is assigned a level of conformance:

  • Level A – The minimum level of accessibility. Failing to meet this level means some users may not be able to access content at all.
  • Level AA – The standard level required by most accessibility regulations. It addresses the most common barriers and provides a more inclusive experience.
  • Level AAA – The highest level of accessibility. It is very strict and not always possible to achieve for all types of content. It is generally used for content that must be highly accessible.

Understanding Techniques and Failures

WCAG provides a set of documented techniques and failures to help developers implement the success criteria effectively:

  • Techniques – These are best practices or recommended methods for meeting a success criterion. They are categorized as sufficient or advisory techniques.
  • Failures – These are practices that violate a specific success criterion. Avoiding these helps ensure accessibility.

While techniques are helpful, they are not mandatory. Developers can use alternative methods as long as the success criteria are met.

How WCAG Relates to Other Accessibility Standards

WCAG is a foundational document that influences laws and standards worldwide. For example:

  • The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in the United States often refers to WCAG as the standard for digital accessibility.
  • The European Union Web Accessibility Directive is based on WCAG 2.1 Level AA.
  • In India, the Guidelines for Indian Government Websites (GIGW) refer to WCAG to ensure accessible content for all citizens.

WCAG also complements other standards like ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications), which provides ways to improve accessibility for dynamic content and advanced user interface controls.

4 Principals of WCAG

Perceivable

Principle 1: Perceivable - Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

This means that users must be able to perceive the information being presented. It addresses the needs of users with visual, auditory, and other sensory disabilities.

Guidelines under Perceivable:
  • Guideline 1.1: Text Alternatives: Provide text alternatives for any non-text content so that it can be changed into other forms people need, such as large print, Braille, speech, symbols or simpler language.
  • Guideline 1.2: Time-based Media: Provide alternatives for time-based media.
  • Guideline 1.3: Adaptable: Create content that can be presented in different ways (for example simpler layout) without losing information or structure.
  • Guideline 1.4: Distinguishable: Make it easier for users to see and hear content including separating foreground from background.

Operable

Principle 2: Operable - User interface components and navigation must be operable.

This means that users must be able to operate the interface. It addresses the needs of users who cannot use a mouse or keyboard, as well as those with time limitations or who experience seizures.

Guidelines under Operable:
  • Guideline 2.1: Keyboard Accessible: Make all functionality available from a keyboard.
  • Guideline 2.2: Enough Time: Provide users enough time to read and use content.
  • Guideline 2.3: Seizures and Physical Reactions: Do not design content in a way that is known to cause seizures or physical reactions.
  • Guideline 2.4: Navigable: Provide ways to help users navigate, find content, and determine where they are.

Understandable

Principle 3: Understandable - Information and the operation of user interface must be understandable.

This means that users must be able to understand both the information and the operation of the user interface. It addresses the needs of users with cognitive disabilities, learning disabilities, and language barriers.

Guidelines under Understandable:
  • Guideline 3.1: Readable: Make text content readable and understandable.
  • Guideline 3.2: Predictable: Make Web pages appear and operate in predictable ways.
  • Guideline 3.3: Input Assistance: Help users avoid and correct mistakes.

Robust

Principle 4: Robust - Content must be robust enough that it can be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

This means that content should be developed using well-structured markup and should conform to accessibility standards to ensure compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies.

Guidelines under Robust:
  • Guideline 4.1: Compatible: Maximize compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies.

1.1.1: Non-text Content

Guideline Name: Text Alternatives

level: A

description:

All non-text content that serves a purpose—such as images, icons, charts, graphical buttons, and media control symbols—must include a text alternative that conveys the same meaning or function. This ensures accessibility for users who rely on assistive technologies like screen readers. Images that convey information, perform actions, or represent meaningful content must have appropriate alt text. Decorative images that do not convey information should have empty alt attributes (alt="") so they can be ignored by screen readers. Complex images like graphs or charts may need a longer text explanation or reference to nearby text. Avoid using images of text unless necessary, and provide the same content in text format whenever possible. Examples of required alt text: logos, action icons (like play or close), informative photos, and charts. Examples where alt="" is acceptable: borders, spacers, purely decorative icons, or background graphics.

level: AA

description:

The content does not restrict its view and operation to a single display orientation, such as portrait or landscape, unless a specific orientation is essential. 1.4.12: Text Spacing

Guideline Name: Distinguishable

Level: AA

Description:

Imagine you need to read a lot of text online, but it's hard for you because the letters, words, or lines are too close together. This rule ensures that websites allow you to increase that spacing to make reading easier. Even if you make the text significantly more spread out—for example, increasing the line height to at least 1.5 times the font size, or adding more space between words (0.16 times), letters (0.12 times), and paragraphs (2 times)—the website's content should still remain clear, readable, and fully functional. This is especially helpful for people with dyslexia, low vision, or cognitive and learning disabilities.

To achieve this, developers should:

  • Use Relative Units: Whenever possible, use flexible units like em, rem, or percentages for font sizes, line heights, and spacing (padding/margins). Avoid fixed units like px for text and its containers, as this allows the layout to adjust when text spacing increases.

  • Prevent Overlapping or Clipping: Ensure that when text spacing is increased up to the required thresholds, no text overlaps other elements, gets cut off, or becomes hidden. All content must remain readable, and interactive elements (like buttons or links) must still be usable.
  • Test Your Design: Use browser extensions or custom style sheets to simulate increased text spacing and confirm that your design remains accessible and functional under these conditions.

2.5.8: Target Size

Guideline Name: Input Modalities

level: AA

description:

Interactive elements like buttons, links, and form controls must have a target size large enough to be easily activated by users.

The minimum target size required by WCAG is at least 24 by 24 CSS pixels. This helps users with limited dexterity, tremors, or those using touch devices to activate controls without difficulty or accidental activation of nearby elements.

Small clickable areas can be hard to tap or click, especially on mobile devices or for users who use assistive devices.

For example, a button that is just 10x10 pixels is very hard to click, while a button of 30x30 pixels or larger is easier.

Practical Test Case: 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum)

Level: AA

Test Scenario: Verify that the size of target for pointer inputs (e.g., buttons, links, interactive elements) is at least 24 by 24 CSS pixels.
Steps to Reproduce:
  1. Identify all interactive target elements on the page that can be activated by a pointer (e.g., buttons, links, form fields, checkboxes, radio buttons, custom controls).
  2. Using browser developer tools (e.g., Inspect Element in Chrome/Firefox) or a measurement tool, measure the actual size of the *interactive area* (hit area) of each target. This includes any padding that contributes to the click/touch area.
  3. Pay particular attention to small icons, inline links, or tightly spaced elements.
  4. Verify that the smallest dimension (height or width) of the target's interactive area is at least 24 CSS pixels.
  5. Note that this applies to the target size, not necessarily the visual size of the icon/text within it.
Expected Result: All interactive target elements have a target size of at least 24 by 24 CSS pixels, or one of the following exceptions applies:
  • Equivalent: The target is available through an equivalent link or control on the same page that meets the 24x24 CSS pixel minimum.
  • Inline: The target is in a sentence or block of text (e.g., a hyperlink within a paragraph).
  • User Agent Control: The size of the target is determined by the user agent and not modified by the author.
  • Essential: A particular presentation of the target is essential to the information being conveyed.

Additionally, if passwords are used, users should be able to easily see or hear what they are typing, and alternative authentication options should always be provided.

ARIA Attributes

Introduction to ARIA

What is ARIA?

ARIA stands for Accessible Rich Internet Applications. It is a set of attributes defined by the W3C’s Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) to make web content and applications more accessible to people with disabilities.

Why is ARIA Important?

Modern websites often use interactive elements such as tabs, sliders, menus, and dialog boxes. These elements may not always be accessible to screen readers and other assistive technologies. ARIA helps bridge this gap by:

  • Providing additional information about the role, state, and properties of elements.
  • Improving navigation for users who rely on screen readers.
  • Making dynamic content and rich user interfaces usable for people with visual, motor, or cognitive impairments.

aria-label

The roleIt’s helpful when there is no visible text but you want screen readers to speak something meaningful. This is often used for icon buttons, input fields, and elements that need a label but don’t have one. The text in aria-label will only be read by assistive technologies like screen readers.

Example: A search button with a magnifying glass icon.

<button aria-label="Search">🔍</button>

aria-labelledby

The aria-labelledby attribute tells the screen reader to use another element’s text as the label. You must give the label element an id, and use that ID in the aria-labelledby attribute. This is useful when an input or section is described by a heading or label already on the page. It's great for reusing visible text as the accessible label.

Example: Input field labeled by a heading.

<h2 id="name-label">Full Name</h2>
<input type="text" aria-labelledby="name-label">

aria-describedby

The aria-describedby attribute is used when you want to give extra information about an element. Screen readers will first read the label, then read the description from the element referenced by this ID. It’s very useful for form inputs, buttons, or anything that needs additional help text or instructions for users.

Example: Input field with helpful description.

<label for="email">Email</label>
<input type="email" id="email" aria-describedby="email-help">
<small id="email-help">We'll never share your email with anyone.</small>

aria-hidden

The aria-hidden attribute hides elements from screen readers. If you set aria-hidden="true", that part of the page will not be read aloud. This is helpful for decorative elements like icons or repeated visual content that doesn’t add meaning. It improves the screen reader experience by reducing unnecessary information.

Example: Star icon hidden from screen reader.

<span aria-hidden="true">★</span>

role

The role attribute defines what kind of element it is, especially when the element is not a native HTML tag like a button or checkbox. It helps screen readers understand the purpose of the element. Use role to make a <div> act like a button, a <span> act like a checkbox, and so on. It's a powerful tool when creating custom UI components.

Example: A div that behaves like a button.

<div role="button" tabindex="0">Click Me</div>

aria-expanded

The aria-expanded attribute tells whether an element (like a dropdown, accordion, or menu) is currently expanded or collapsed. Screen readers will announce this to help users know if extra content is visible. This is very useful for buttons that show or hide content when clicked.

Example: A button that toggles additional content.

<button aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="details">
  Show Details
</button>
<div id="details" hidden>
  Here are the details!
</div>

aria-checked

The aria-checked attribute is used with custom checkbox elements. It tells screen readers whether the item is selected (true), not selected (false), or mixed. This is useful when building custom toggles or checkbox-like elements that are not standard HTML checkboxes.

Example: A custom checkbox div with selection status.

<div role="checkbox" aria-checked="true" tabindex="0">
  Subscribe to Newsletter
</div>

aria-pressed

The aria-pressed attribute is used on toggle buttons. It lets screen reader users know whether the button is currently in a pressed (active) or unpressed (inactive) state. This is helpful for bold/italic buttons in text editors or like/dislike buttons.

Example: A toggle button for bold text.

<button aria-pressed="false">Bold</button>

Aria-Live

The aria-live attribute is used for dynamic content updates. It tells screen readers to automatically read changes in a region, even if the user’s focus is somewhere else. This is helpful for alerts, timers, or chat messages. You can use values like "polite", "assertive", or "off".

Example: A polite live region that updates without interrupting.

<div aria-live="polite">
  New message received!
</div>

aria-relevant

The aria-relevant attribute is used with live regions to specify what types of changes should be announced to assistive technologies, such as screen readers. It helps control how users are notified about updates in dynamic content.

Possible values:

  • additions – Announce only new nodes added.
  • removals – Announce when nodes are removed.
  • text – Announce text changes.
  • all – Announce all changes (additions, removals, text updates).

Example: Live chat messages announcing new entries only.


<div role="log" aria-live="polite" aria-relevant="additions">
  <div>User1: Hello!</div>
  <div>User2: Hi, how are you?</div>
</div>

aria-atomic

The aria-atomic attribute is used with live regions to control whether assistive technologies should present the entire region or only the changed part when content updates.

Possible values:

  • false – Only the changed content inside the live region is announced (default).
  • true – The entire region is announced, even if only part of it changes.

Example: Weather update with entire region announcement.


<div aria-live="polite" aria-atomic="true">
  <h4>Weather Update</h4>
  <p>Temperature: 30°C</p>
  <p>Condition: Sunny</p>
</div>

aria-disabled

The aria-disabled attribute tells assistive technology that an element is disabled, even if it is not visually shown as such. Unlike the HTML disabled attribute, aria-disabled works on more elements and gives developers more control over how things appear vs. behave.

Example: A button that appears clickable but is announced as disabled.

<button aria-disabled="true">Submit</button>

aria-required

The aria-required attribute tells assistive technologies that a form field must be filled out before submitting. It is useful for custom form fields that don’t use the native required HTML attribute. Screen readers will announce that the field is required, helping users understand form rules clearly.

Example: A custom input field marked as required.

<input type="text" aria-required="true" aria-label="First Name">

aria-current

The aria-current attribute indicates the current item in a list, navigation, or set of tabs. It helps screen reader users know which item is currently active or selected. Common values are "page", "step", or "true". It is mostly used in navigation menus to highlight the current page.

Example: Highlighting the current page in a navigation list.

<a href="home.html" aria-current="page">Home</a>

aria-haspopup

The aria-haspopup attribute shows that an element will open a popup like a menu, dialog, or listbox. This helps screen reader users prepare for a new type of interaction. You can also set values like "menu", "listbox", "dialog", or "grid" to describe the popup type.

Example: A button that opens a menu.

<button aria-haspopup="true" aria-controls="menu1">
  Options
</button>

aria-busy

The aria-busy attribute tells assistive technologies that content is loading or being updated. When aria-busy="true", screen readers will wait before reading changes in that region. It is very helpful for dynamic areas like search results or dashboards that load data in real-time.

Example: A content area that is busy loading.

<div aria-busy="true">
  Loading your dashboard...
</div>

aria-level

The aria-level attribute defines the heading level for an element, especially when it's not using a native <h1> to <h6> tag. It tells screen readers the importance or rank of the heading. This is useful when using styled text or <div> elements for headings.

Example: A div used as a level 2 heading.

<div role="heading" aria-level="2">
  Section Title
</div>

aria-selected

The aria-selected attribute indicates if an item in a group (like a tab or a listbox) is currently selected. It helps screen reader users know which item is active among others. This is useful in tab interfaces, selectable lists, or grids where visual selection is shown but needs to be announced accessibly.

Example: One tab is selected among multiple.

<div role="tablist">
  <button role="tab" aria-selected="true">Overview</button>
  <button role="tab" aria-selected="false">Details</button>
</div>

aria-controls

The aria-controls attribute identifies the ID of the element that is being controlled by another element, like a button controlling a panel. It helps users understand which area will change when they interact with the button or control.

Example: A button that shows or hides a section.

<button aria-controls="info-panel">
  Toggle Information
</button>

<div id="info-panel">
  Additional information is shown here.
</div>

role="alert"

The role="alert" makes content automatically announced by screen readers as soon as it appears. This is useful for error messages or important notices that users should know about immediately without moving their focus.

Example: Displaying an error message.

<div role="alert">
  Error: You must fill out all required fields.
</div>

role="dialog"

The role="dialog" is used for modal or popup dialog boxes. It lets screen reader users know they’re inside a dialog and should not interact with the rest of the page until it’s closed. It's often paired with aria-modal="true" and focus trapping.

Example: A modal popup with content.

<div role="dialog" aria-modal="true">
  Are you sure you want to delete this item?
</div>

aria-modal

The aria-modal attribute is used with role="dialog" to tell screen readers that the dialog is modal. This means users should interact only with the dialog content until it's closed. It prevents screen readers from reading other content in the background.

Example: A modal dialog that blocks interaction with the page.

<div role="dialog" aria-modal="true">
  Please confirm your action before continuing.
</div>

aria-owns

The aria-owns attribute defines a parent–child relationship between elements when that relationship cannot be determined from the DOM structure alone. It allows authors to override the accessibility tree to include elements that are elsewhere in the DOM as children of the current element.

Important notes:

  • Used when the DOM order does not match the logical relationship for assistive technologies.
  • Should be used carefully, as it can cause confusion if misapplied.
  • It does not move elements visually, only in the accessibility tree.

Example: Associating menu items with a menu container.


<div role="menu" aria-owns="item1 item2">
  Main Menu
</div>

<div id="item1" role="menuitem">Profile</div>
<div id="item2" role="menuitem">Settings</div>

aria-readonly

The aria-readonly attribute tells assistive technologies that a form control is read-only. The user can read the content but cannot change it. This helps screen reader users understand they cannot edit the field.

Example: A read-only input field.

<input type="text" value="Not Editable" aria-readonly="true">

aria-activedescendant

The aria-activedescendant attribute is used in composite widgets like autocomplete or listbox to show which item is currently active. It helps assistive technologies track the focus without moving the actual input focus.

Example: Keyboard navigation through suggestions.

<input type="text" aria-activedescendant="option2" aria-owns="listbox1">
<ul id="listbox1">
  <li id="option1">Apple</li>
  <li id="option2">Banana</li>
</ul>

aria-valuemin, aria-valuemax, aria-valuenow

These attributes are used in widgets like sliders or progress bars. aria-valuemin and aria-valuemax set the range, while aria-valuenow tells the current value. This allows screen reader users to understand how far along a process is.

Example: A progress bar at 70%.

<div role="progressbar"
     aria-valuemin="0"
     aria-valuemax="100"
     aria-valuenow="70">
  70% Complete
</div>

aria-orientation

The aria-orientation attribute defines whether a widget like a tab list or slider is arranged vertically or horizontally. This helps screen readers announce how the items are displayed so users can navigate accordingly.

Example: Horizontal tab list.

<div role="tablist" aria-orientation="horizontal">
  <div role="tab">Tab 1</div>
  <div role="tab">Tab 2</div>
</div>

aria-multiselectable

Used on a listbox or grid, aria-multiselectable tells screen readers if the user can select more than one item. This is useful in scenarios where users need to select multiple options like in filters or checklists.

Example: Listbox that supports multiple selection.

<ul role="listbox" aria-multiselectable="true">
  <li role="option">Option A</li>
  <li role="option">Option B</li>
</ul>

aria-sort

The aria-sort attribute is used on table headers to indicate the sorting direction: ascending, descending, none, or other. This is very helpful for screen reader users to know how data is sorted.

Example: Column sorted in descending order.

<th aria-sort="descending">Price</th>

aria-dropeffect

aria-dropeffect was used to show what kind of drop action would be allowed in a drag-and-drop interface (e.g., copy, move, link). This attribute is now deprecated, and better alternatives should be used with HTML5 drag and drop.

Example: (Deprecated usage)

<div aria-dropeffect="move">Drop files here</div>

aria-grabbed

The aria-grabbed attribute was used in older ARIA specifications to indicate whether an element is currently grabbed for drag-and-drop operations. However, it is now deprecated in ARIA 1.1, and authors are encouraged to use modern drag-and-drop APIs instead.

Possible values:

  • true – The element is currently grabbed (selected for dragging).
  • false – The element is not grabbed (default).

Example: Draggable list item marked as grabbed.


<div role="listitem" draggable="true" aria-grabbed="true">
  File1.txt (dragging)
</div>

aria-keyshortcuts

The aria-keyshortcuts attribute tells assistive technologies what keyboard shortcut activates the element. It allows users to know that a specific key combo like "Ctrl+S" will perform an action.

Example: Button triggered with Ctrl+S.

<button aria-keyshortcuts="Ctrl+S">Save</button>

aria-flowto

The aria-flowto attribute specifies the next element in the reading flow. It’s used when the visual order differs from the logical DOM order, helping screen readers follow the correct reading sequence.

Example: Directing screen readers to jump to another section.

<div id="box1" aria-flowto="box2">Step 1</div>
<div id="box2">Step 2</div>

aria-autocomplete

Used in input fields, aria-autocomplete tells screen readers if autocomplete suggestions are available. It can have values like inline, list, or both, helping users understand how they can choose suggestions.

Example: Autocomplete for a search bar.

<input aria-autocomplete="list" aria-owns="suggestions">
<ul id="suggestions">
  <li>Apple</li>
  <li>Banana</li>
</ul>

aria-colspan

aria-colspan defines how many columns a cell should span in a grid. It helps screen readers understand the structure of complex grids, just like the HTML colspan attribute in tables.

Example: A cell spanning two columns.

<div role="gridcell" aria-colspan="2">Merged Column</div>

aria-rowspan

The aria-rowspan attribute defines how many rows a cell spans in a grid or table structure. It works similarly to the HTML rowspan and helps screen readers interpret table layout correctly.

Example: A cell spanning two rows.

<div role="gridcell" aria-rowspan="2">Merged Row</div>

aria-colindex

aria-colindex tells assistive technologies the column number for a grid or table cell. This is helpful when table headers are complex and don’t appear in the usual DOM order.

Example: A cell at column 3.

<div role="gridcell" aria-colindex="3">Data</div>

aria-rowindex

The aria-rowindex attribute tells screen readers the row number of a cell in a table or grid. It is useful for creating accessible data grids with proper navigation for screen reader users.

Example: A cell in row 2.

<div role="gridcell" aria-rowindex="2">Item</div>

aria-placeholder

aria-placeholder provides placeholder text for assistive technologies when the native HTML placeholder isn't enough or is visually hidden. It describes the expected input.

Example: Input for entering a city name.

<input type="text" aria-placeholder="Enter your city">

aria-posinset

The aria-posinset attribute defines an element’s position in a set. It helps users understand their current position among siblings (like "3 of 5").

Example: Item 2 of 4 in a list.

<li role="option" aria-posinset="2" aria-setsize="4">Option 2</li>

aria-setsize

aria-setsize tells assistive technologies the total number of items in a set or list. When used with aria-posinset, it helps screen readers provide context like “item 2 of 5”.

Example: Set of 4 options.

<li role="option" aria-posinset="1" aria-setsize="4">Option 1</li>

aria-braillelabel

This attribute gives a label specifically for braille displays. It may differ from the spoken label and helps provide better accessibility for braille readers.

Example: Label for a braille display.


<button aria-label="Submit form" aria-braillelabel="Braille: Submit">
  Send
</button>

aria-brailleroledescription

This attribute customizes the role name spoken or shown on a braille display. It is useful when the default role name is too long or not meaningful for braille users.

Example: Shortened braille role for a switch.


<div role="switch" aria-brailleroledescription="Sw">On/Off</div>

aria-errormessage

aria-errormessage provides a reference to an error message element that explains what's wrong with the input. This improves form validation messages for screen readers.

Example: Input with an error message.


<input id="email" aria-invalid="true" aria-errormessage="error1">
<div id="error1">Please enter a valid email address.</div>

aria-roledescription

This gives a custom role description that screen readers announce. It replaces the default role name with something more meaningful or familiar to users.

Example: Role description for a custom slider.


<div role="slider" aria-roledescription="volume control"></div>

aria-details

The aria-details attribute links to another element that provides more detailed information. It’s useful for charts, summaries, and tooltips.

Example: Element with additional explanation.


<div aria-details="info1">COVID Data</div>
<div id="info1">This data shows infection rates from Jan to March.</div>

aria-valuetext

The aria-valuetext attribute defines a human-readable text alternative for the current value of a widget, such as a slider, spin button, or progress bar. It is especially useful when numeric values need to be expressed in words or more descriptive terms.

Example 1: Slider with descriptive value text for volume control.


<input type="range" 
       min="0" 
       max="100" 
       value="75" 
       role="slider" 
       aria-valuemin="0" 
       aria-valuemax="100" 
       aria-valuenow="75" 
       aria-valuetext="High volume">

Example 2: Temperature slider with descriptive value text.


<input type="range" 
       min="0" 
       max="40" 
       value="30" 
       role="slider" 
       aria-valuemin="0" 
       aria-valuemax="40" 
       aria-valuenow="30" 
       aria-valuetext="Warm">

aria-invalid

The aria-invalid attribute indicates whether the value entered into an input field is invalid or contains errors. It helps assistive technologies notify users when a field requires correction.

Possible values:

  • false – The field value is valid (default).
  • true – The field value is invalid.
  • grammar – The field has a grammatical error.
  • spelling – The field contains a spelling mistake.

Example 1: Text input marked invalid (email format error).


<input type="email" 
       value="user@@example.com" 
       aria-invalid="true">

Example 2: Password field with weak password.


<input type="password" 
       value="1234" 
       aria-invalid="true" 
       aria-describedby="pwdHelp">
<div id="pwdHelp">Password too weak. Use at least 8 characters.</div>

Example 3: Input with spelling mistake indication.


<input type="text" 
       value="Acessibility" 
       aria-invalid="spelling">

aria-posinset

The aria-posinset attribute defines the position of an item within a set of related elements. It is typically used together with aria-setsize to help assistive technologies convey list or tree structures when the DOM alone does not provide enough information.

Important notes:

  • It is a 1-based index (first item is 1, second is 2, etc.).
  • Commonly used with role="listitem", role="treeitem", or similar grouped roles.
  • Should be used when items are dynamically generated, hidden, or not sequential in the DOM.

Example: List items with explicit position in a set.


<ul role="list">
  <li role="listitem" aria-posinset="1" aria-setsize="3">Apple</li>
  <li role="listitem" aria-posinset="2" aria-setsize="3">Banana</li>
  <li role="listitem" aria-posinset="3" aria-setsize="3">Cherry</li>
</ul>

role="button"

The button role identifies an element as a button, which can be pressed by the user to perform an action. Native HTML <button> elements already have this role by default, but it can be applied to custom elements like <div> or <span> when needed.

Important notes:

  • Users expect it to be focusable with Tab and operable with Enter or Space.
  • Use native <button> when possible for built-in accessibility.

Example: Custom div as a button


<div role="button" tabindex="0">
  Submit Form
</div>

role="checkbox"

The checkbox role identifies an element as a checkbox that can be checked, unchecked, or mixed. Native HTML checkboxes already have this role by default, but it can be applied to custom elements when building accessible widgets.

Important notes:

  • Use aria-checked to indicate state (true, false, or mixed).
  • Ensure the element is focusable and togglable with the Space key.

Example: Custom checkbox


<div role="checkbox" tabindex="0" aria-checked="false">
  I agree to the terms
</div>

role="link"

The link role identifies an element as a link that navigates to another page or resource. Native HTML <a href> already has this role by default.

Important notes:

  • Should be keyboard operable with Enter.
  • If using a custom element, ensure it has tabindex="0".

Example: Custom link


<div role="link" tabindex="0">
  Go to Home Page
</div>

role="menuitem"

The menuitem role identifies an element as an item inside a menu. It is usually a child of role="menu" or role="menubar".

Important notes:

  • Use in combination with role="menu".
  • Can also use menuitemcheckbox or menuitemradio for specific states.

Example: Menu with items


<div role="menu">
  <div role="menuitem">New File</div>
  <div role="menuitem">Save</div>
</div>

role="radio"

The radio role identifies an element as a radio button within a group. It should be used inside a radiogroup.

Important notes:

  • Use aria-checked to indicate selection (true or false).
  • Should be keyboard operable with Arrow keys inside a group.

Example: Custom radio buttons


<div role="radiogroup">
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="true" tabindex="0">Male</div>
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="false" tabindex="-1">Female</div>
</div>

role="switch"

The switch role identifies a toggle control that represents on/off states. It is similar to a checkbox but indicates a different kind of interaction (like a light switch).

Important notes:

  • State is managed using aria-checked (true or false).
  • Keyboard activation should use Space.

Example: Custom switch


<div role="switch" tabindex="0" aria-checked="false">
  Dark Mode
</div>

role="tab"

The tab role identifies an element as a tab in a tabbed interface. It is usually part of a tablist and controls a tabpanel.

Important notes:

  • Selected tab should have aria-selected="true".
  • Keyboard navigation uses Arrow keys.

Example: Tab list


<div role="tablist">
  <div role="tab" aria-selected="true">Home</div>
  <div role="tab" aria-selected="false">Profile</div>
</div>

role="tabpanel"

The tabpanel role defines the content area associated with a tab in a tabbed interface. It is controlled by a role="tab".

Important notes:

  • Should have aria-labelledby pointing to its controlling tab.
  • Only the active panel should be visible.

Example: Tab panel


<div role="tabpanel" aria-labelledby="tab1">
  Home content goes here.
</div>

role="textbox"

The textbox role identifies an element as an input field for text. Native HTML text inputs and textareas already have this role by default.

Important notes:

  • Use aria-multiline="true" if the textbox accepts multiple lines (like a textarea).
  • Ensure the element is editable and keyboard accessible.

Example: Custom text box


<div role="textbox" contenteditable="true">
  Type here...
</div>

role="treeitem"

The treeitem role identifies an element as an item in a hierarchical tree structure. It is usually a child of role="tree" or another treeitem.

Important notes:

  • Use aria-expanded to indicate if the item is expanded or collapsed.
  • Keyboard navigation typically uses Arrow keys.

Example: Tree with items


<ul role="tree">
  <li role="treeitem" aria-expanded="true">Documents</li>
  <li role="treeitem">Pictures</li>
</ul>

Role: listitem

The listitem role represents a single item in a list. It should be contained within elements with role="list" or role="group".

Example:


<ul role="list">
  <li role="listitem">First item</li>
  <li role="listitem">Second item</li>
</ul>

Role: log

The log role is used for content that changes frequently, like chat logs or live updates. Screen readers automatically announce new content.

Example:


<div role="log">
  <p>User1: Hello!</p>
  <p>User2: Hi there!</p>
</div>

Role: main

The main role identifies the main content area of a page. Each page should only have one main region.

Example:


<main role="main">
  <h1>Welcome to Accessible World</h1>
  <p>This is the main content of the page.</p>
</main>

Role: marquee

The marquee role is used for scrolling or moving text. However, it is rarely recommended for accessibility since moving text can be distracting.

Example:


<div role="marquee">
  Breaking News: Accessibility matters!
</div>

Role: math

The math role is used to indicate that the content represents a mathematical expression.

Example:


<div role="math">
  E = mc<sup>2</sup>
</div>

Role: menu

The menu role is used for a group of commands or options presented to the user, often in navigation or context menus.

Example:


<ul role="menu">
  <li role="menuitem">New File</li>
  <li role="menuitem">Open File</li>
</ul>

Role: menubar

The menubar role represents a horizontal menu, usually placed at the top of an application window or page.

Example:


<div role="menubar">
  <div role="menuitem">File</div>
  <div role="menuitem">Edit</div>
</div>

Role: menuitem

The menuitem role is used for items within a menu that a user can activate.

Example:


<ul role="menu">
  <li role="menuitem">Save</li>
  <li role="menuitem">Close</li>
</ul>

Role: menuitemcheckbox

The menuitemcheckbox role is a menu item that behaves like a checkbox. It can be checked or unchecked within a menu.

Example:


<ul role="menu">
  <li role="menuitemcheckbox" aria-checked="true">Show Line Numbers</li>
</ul>

Role: menuitemradio

The menuitemradio role is a menu item that acts like a radio button, allowing one option to be selected from a group.

Example:


<ul role="menu">
  <li role="menuitemradio" aria-checked="true">Light Theme</li>
  <li role="menuitemradio" aria-checked="false">Dark Theme</li>
</ul>

Role: navigation

The navigation role identifies a section of a page that provides links for navigating the website.

Example:


<nav role="navigation">
  <a href="#home">Home</a>
  <a href="#about">About</a>
</nav>

Role: none

The none role removes the semantic meaning of an element for assistive technologies, making it purely presentational.

Example:


<div role="none">Decorative box</div>

Role: note

The note role indicates additional information or annotations that may be useful for the user.

Example:


<div role="note">
  Remember to save your work frequently.
</div>

Role: option

The option role is used for selectable items inside a listbox, representing one choice from a set of options.

Example:


<ul role="listbox">
  <li role="option">Option 1</li>
  <li role="option">Option 2</li>
</ul>

Role: presentation

The presentation role makes an element ignored by assistive technologies, similar to role="none". It’s used for layout-only elements.

Example:


<table role="presentation">
  <tr><td>Layout only</td></tr>
</table>

Role: progressbar

The progressbar role is used to indicate the progress of a task, such as file download or loading.

Example:


<div role="progressbar" aria-valuenow="50" aria-valuemin="0" aria-valuemax="100">
  50% Complete
</div>

Role: radio

The radio role represents a single radio button in a group, allowing only one selection at a time.

Example:


<div role="radiogroup">
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="true">Option A</div>
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="false">Option B</div>
</div>

Role: radiogroup

The radiogroup role groups related radio buttons together, ensuring only one can be selected within the group.

Example:


<div role="radiogroup">
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="true">Male</div>
  <div role="radio" aria-checked="false">Female</div>
</div>

Role: region

The region role identifies a perceivable section of a page that is important enough to be included in a page summary or landmark navigation.

Example:


<div role="region" aria-label="User Profile">
  <h2>Profile Information</h2>
</div>

Role: row

The row role represents a row of cells within a table, grid, or treegrid.

Example:


<table role="grid">
  <tr role="row">
    <td role="gridcell">Data 1</td>
    <td role="gridcell">Data 2</td>
  </tr>
</table>

role="switch"

The switch role represents a toggle control that can be either "on" or "off". It is similar to a checkbox but indicates a different kind of binary option.

Example: Dark mode toggle.


<div role="switch" aria-checked="false">Dark Mode</div>

role="tab"

The tab role is used for items in a tablist that switch between panels of content.

Example: A tab control.


<div role="tab" aria-selected="true">Overview</div>
<div role="tab" aria-selected="false">Details</div>

role="tablist"

The tablist role groups together a set of tabs.

Example: Tab container.


<div role="tablist">
  <div role="tab">Home</div>
  <div role="tab">Profile</div>
</div>

role="tabpanel"

The tabpanel role defines the content area associated with a tab.

Example: Panel of tab content.


<div role="tabpanel">This is the Home tab content.</div>

role="term"

The term role identifies a word or phrase being defined in a definition list.

Example: Definition term.


<div role="term">HTML</div>
<div role="definition">A markup language for web pages.</div>

role="textbox"

The textbox role defines an input field that allows users to enter free text.

Example: Search field.


<div role="textbox" aria-multiline="false">Type here...</div>

role="timer"

The timer role represents a numerical counter or stopwatch that updates over time.

Example: Countdown timer.


<div role="timer" aria-live="polite">10 seconds left</div>

role="toolbar"

The toolbar role groups interactive controls such as buttons and menus.

Example: Text editor toolbar.


<div role="toolbar">
  <button>Bold</button>
  <button>Italic</button>
</div>

role="tooltip"

The tooltip role is used for a popup label that provides extra information about an element when hovered or focused.

Example: Help tooltip.


<div role="tooltip">Enter your email address.</div>

role="tree"

The tree role represents a hierarchical list of items, such as a file explorer.

Example: File tree structure.


<ul role="tree">
  <li role="treeitem">Folder 1</li>
  <li role="treeitem">Folder 2</li>
</ul>

role="treegrid"

The treegrid role combines tree and grid, useful for data that is hierarchical and tabular.

Example: Expandable data grid.


<div role="treegrid">Project Data</div>

role="treeitem"

The treeitem role defines an item within a tree structure.

Example: File item in a tree.


<div role="treeitem">index.html</div>

role="menuitemcheckbox"

The menuitemcheckbox role defines a menu item that can be checked or unchecked.

Example: Menu with options.


<div role="menuitemcheckbox" aria-checked="true">Show Toolbar</div>

role="menuitemradio"

The menuitemradio role defines a menu item that is part of a group where only one can be selected.

Example: Font size options.


<div role="menuitemradio" aria-checked="true">Small</div>
<div role="menuitemradio" aria-checked="false">Large</div>

role="note"

The note role is used for content that is an annotation or advisory note.

Example: Side note.


<div role="note">Note: Changes will be saved automatically.</div>

role="math"

The math role indicates a mathematical expression, often rendered with MathML.

Example: Formula display.


<div role="math">E = mc²</div>

role="log"

The log role is for dynamic content where new entries are added and old entries disappear, like chat or error logs.

Example: Chat log.


<div role="log">User joined the chat.</div>

role="marquee"

The marquee role is used for content that scrolls or moves across the screen.

Example: Scrolling news.


<div role="marquee">Breaking News: Accessibility updates released.</div>

role="status"

The status role is for advisory messages that are not critical, such as progress updates.

<div role="status">Your document has been saved.</div>

role="alertdialog"

The alertdialog role represents a dialog box that interrupts the user with important information and requires acknowledgment.

<div role="alertdialog">Are you sure you want to delete this file?</div>

role="article"

The article role represents a self-contained piece of content intended for independent use or distribution.

<div role="article">
  <h2>Breaking News</h2>

role="banner"

The banner role represents a site-wide header that usually contains the logo or main identity.

<header role="banner">

How to Perform Accessibility Testing Using Axe DevTools Add-on

Skip to video lesson

Step 1: Install the Axe DevTools Extension

  • Open your Chrome or Firefox browser.
  • Go to the web store and search for "Axe DevTools" or click on this link.
  • Click on "Add to Chrome" or "Add to Firefox".
  • Once installed, you will see the axe icon in the browser toolbar.

Step 2: Open the Web Page You Want to Test

  • Navigate to any webpage you want to check for accessibility issues.
  • Press F12 or Ctrl + Shift + I to open Developer Tools.
  • Find and click on the "axe DevTools" tab inside Developer Tools.

Step 3: Run the Accessibility Test

  • In the axe DevTools tab, click the button that says "Scan ALL of my page".
  • Wait a few seconds while axe scans the page.

Step 4: Review the Results

  • Axe will show you a list of accessibility issues found on the page.
  • Each issue includes:
    • A description of the problem
    • Why it matters
    • How to fix it (with code examples)

Step 5: Fix and Re-test

  • Use the suggestions provided by axe to fix accessibility issues in your code.
  • After making changes, refresh the page and run the test again to confirm the issues are resolved.

Benefits of Using Axe DevTools:

  • Free and easy to use
  • Helpful for beginners in accessibility testing
  • Works directly in your browser

How to Perform Accessibility Testing Using WAVE Tool

Skip to video lesson

Step 1: Install the WAVE Browser Extension

  • Open your Chrome or Firefox browser.
  • Go to the web store and search for "WAVE Evaluation Tool".
  • Click "Add to Chrome" or "Add to Firefox" to install the extension.
  • Once installed, you will see the WAVE icon in your browser toolbar.

Step 2: Open the Web Page You Want to Test

  • Navigate to the webpage you want to check for accessibility issues.
  • Click the WAVE icon in your browser toolbar.

Step 3: View the Accessibility Report

  • The page will reload with WAVE’s overlay showing icons and panels.
  • You will see:
    • Errors – accessibility issues that must be fixed
    • Alerts – possible issues to review
    • Features – elements that improve accessibility
    • Structural elements – headings, landmarks, etc.
    • ARIA usage – if present

Step 4: Understand and Fix Issues

  • Click on any icon or item in the sidebar to learn more about that issue.
  • WAVE provides a description and suggestions to fix each problem.
  • Use this information to improve your HTML or ARIA code.

Step 5: Re-Test the Page

  • After making changes to your website, reload the page.
  • Click the WAVE icon again to run another accessibility scan.
  • Continue fixing issues until the page has no critical errors.

Benefits of Using WAVE Tool:

  • Free and easy to use
  • Does not require programming skills
  • Great for visually identifying accessibility issues

How to Perform Accessibility Testing Using Lighthouse

Skip to video lesson

What is Lighthouse?

Lighthouse is an open-source tool built into Google Chrome. It helps you check a website's performance, accessibility, SEO, and more.

Step 1: Open the Web Page You Want to Test

  • Open Google Chrome browser.
  • Go to the website or web page you want to test.

Step 2: Open Chrome Developer Tools

  • Press F12 or Ctrl + Shift + I to open Developer Tools.
  • Click on the "Lighthouse" tab in the Developer Tools panel. If it's not visible, click the ">>" menu to find it.

Step 3: Configure the Report

  • Select the categories you want to test. Make sure Accessibility is checked.
  • Choose the device type: Mobile or Desktop.
  • Click the "Analyze Page Load" or "Generate Report" button.

Step 4: View the Accessibility Report

  • Lighthouse will run the test and generate a report.
  • Look at the Accessibility section in the report.
  • It will show a score out of 100 and list the accessibility issues found.
  • Each issue includes a description and how to fix it.

Step 5: Fix and Re-test

  • Update your webpage based on the suggestions provided.
  • Re-run the Lighthouse test to see if the issues are resolved.

Benefits of Using Lighthouse:

  • Free and built into Chrome
  • No need to install any extension
  • Gives detailed and easy-to-understand reports
