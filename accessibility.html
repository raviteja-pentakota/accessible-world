What is Web Accessibility

Web Accessibility means designing websites that everyone can use, including people with disabilities.

Some people may not see, hear, or move in the same way as others, so websites should be easy for them too.

For example, blind users use screen readers to hear the content of a page.

Web Accessibility includes adding text for images, using proper color contrast, and allowing keyboard navigation.

Making your website accessible helps more people use it and shows you care about equal access for all.

Why is Accessibility Important?

Accessibility is important because everyone should be able to use websites, apps, and online services.

People with disabilities may face problems if websites are not made in an accessible way.

When websites are accessible, it helps blind users, people with low vision, deaf users, and many others.

Accessible design makes the internet fair and usable for everyone, not just for people without disabilities.

It also improves the overall user experience and follows legal and ethical standards.

Benefits of Accessible Websites

Accessible websites offer many benefits for users and businesses.

When a website is accessible, more people can use it easily — including people who are blind, deaf, or have difficulty using a mouse or reading text.

Over 1 billion people in the world live with some kind of disability. By making your website accessible, you open your content to a much larger audience.

Businesses with accessible websites can attract more customers, improve customer satisfaction, and increase profits.

Search engines like Google also favor accessible websites, which helps your website appear higher in search results.

There are also legal reasons to make websites accessible. Many countries, like the United States, follow laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

If a website is not accessible, the business can face legal action, heavy fines, and damage to its reputation.

For example, if an online shopping site is not accessible, blind users may not be able to browse products or complete a purchase.

This means the business loses customers and money. On the other hand, an accessible shopping site can be used by everyone, including people using screen readers or keyboard navigation.

Accessible sites also load better, work on all devices, and give a better experience to all users — not just people with disabilities.

In short, making your website accessible is good for people, good for business, and good for the law.

Legal and Ethical Aspects of Accessibility

Making websites accessible is both a legal requirement and a moral responsibility in many parts of the world.

Many countries have specific laws that require websites to be accessible to people with disabilities.

These laws are in place to make sure everyone has equal access to digital content, services, and online tools.

If businesses or organizations do not follow these laws, they may face legal action or financial penalties.

Ethically, web accessibility is about fairness and giving everyone the same opportunity to use the internet.

Following accessibility guidelines, like the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines), helps create a better and more inclusive web for all.

Introduction to Accessibility Standards and Guidelines

Accessibility standards and guidelines help developers and designers make websites usable for everyone, including people with disabilities.

The most important and widely used guideline is called WCAG – Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

These guidelines are created by the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) to make web content easier to see, hear, and use.

Other important standards like ARIA and Section 508 also support accessibility by guiding how websites and apps should behave.

Following these standards ensures that websites are clear, simple to navigate, and work with assistive technologies like screen readers.

Types of Disabilities and Their Assistive Technologies

Visual Impairments

Visual impairment encompasses a range of conditions affecting eyesight, from low vision to complete blindness. It can be caused by various factors, including congenital conditions, diseases, and injuries. Understanding the different types of visual impairments is crucial for creating accessible web content.

Types of Visual Impairments:
  • Blindness: Complete or near-complete loss of vision. Users rely heavily on non-visual means to access information.
  • Low Vision: Reduced visual acuity that cannot be fully corrected with glasses or contact lenses. Users may benefit from screen magnification, high contrast, and alternative text sizes.
  • Color Blindness (Color Vision Deficiency): Difficulty or inability to distinguish certain colors. Design should not rely solely on color to convey important information.
Assistive Technologies for Visual Impairments:
  • Screen Readers: Software that reads aloud the text and describes non-textual elements (images, buttons, etc.) on a computer screen. Examples include JAWS, NVDA, and VoiceOver.
  • Screen Magnifiers: Software that enlarges a portion of the screen, making it easier for users with low vision to see content.
  • Braille Displays: Hardware devices that translate text into Braille, allowing blind users to read digital content.
  • Voice Recognition Software: Enables users to control their computer and input text using voice commands.
  • High Contrast Themes: Operating system or browser settings that increase the color contrast, improving readability for users with low vision.

Auditory Impairments

Auditory impairment refers to a partial or total inability to hear. This can range from mild hearing loss to profound deafness and can significantly impact how users interact with multimedia content on the web.

Types of Auditory Impairments:
  • Deafness: A profound loss of hearing, often requiring communication through sign language or visual aids.
  • Hard of Hearing: Mild to moderate hearing loss, where users may have difficulty hearing soft sounds or understanding speech, especially in noisy environments.
Assistive Technologies for Auditory Impairments:
  • Captions and Subtitles: Textual representations of the audio content in videos and audio recordings, synchronized with the media.
  • Transcripts: Text versions of audio content, providing access to information for users who cannot hear the audio.
  • Sign Language Interpretation (Video): Videos of sign language interpreters can be embedded alongside audio content.
  • Hearing Aids: Electronic devices that amplify sound for individuals with hearing loss. While not directly a web technology, accessible design considers their use.
  • Visual Alerts: Instead of or in addition to auditory cues, visual notifications can be used to alert users to events.

Motor Impairments

Motor impairments involve limitations in physical movement, coordination, or dexterity. These can be caused by conditions such as cerebral palsy, multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injuries, arthritis, or repetitive strain injuries. Accessible web design ensures that users with motor impairments can navigate and interact with content effectively.

Types of Motor Impairments:
  • Paralysis: Complete or partial loss of muscle function in one or more parts of the body.
  • Weakness: Reduced muscle strength, making it difficult to perform certain movements.
  • Tremors: Involuntary shaking movements that can affect fine motor control.
  • Lack of Coordination: Difficulty controlling and coordinating movements.
Assistive Technologies for Motor Impairments:
  • Keyboard Navigation: Allowing users to navigate and operate all interactive elements using only the keyboard (Tab key, Arrow keys, Enter key, Spacebar).
  • Switch Devices: Input devices that can be activated by a single movement (e.g., pressing a button, puffing air), often used with on-screen keyboards or scanning techniques.
  • Eye-Tracking Devices: Allow users to control the computer cursor and interact with the screen using their eye movements.
  • Head Pointers: Devices worn on the head that allow users to control the cursor by moving their head.
  • Voice Recognition Software: Enables hands-free control of the computer and text input.
  • On-Screen Keyboards: Virtual keyboards that can be operated with a mouse, trackball, switch device, or touch screen.

Cognitive Impairments

Cognitive impairments affect mental functions such as memory, attention, language processing, problem-solving, and comprehension. These can include learning disabilities (like dyslexia), attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), autism spectrum disorder, and memory loss due to aging or injury. Accessible web design aims to make content clear, organized, and easy to understand for users with cognitive differences.

Types of Cognitive Impairments:
  • Learning Disabilities (e.g., Dyslexia, Dysgraphia): Affect how individuals process and learn information, particularly related to reading, writing, and spelling.
  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): Characterized by difficulties with attention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD): A range of conditions characterized by challenges with social interaction, communication, and repetitive behaviors; sensory sensitivities can also be present.
  • Memory Impairments: Difficulties with recalling information, which can be caused by aging, brain injury, or conditions like dementia.
Assistive Technologies and Strategies for Cognitive Impairments:
  • Screen Readers (for text-to-speech): Can aid users with dyslexia or reading difficulties.
  • Text-to-Speech Tools: Software that reads selected text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Tools: Can help users who struggle with writing.
  • Simplified Language and Layout: Using clear, concise language and consistent, uncluttered layouts.
  • Visual Aids and Multimedia: Incorporating images, videos, and diagrams to support understanding.
  • Consistent Navigation: Providing clear and predictable navigation structures.
  • পর্যাপ্ত সময়সীমা (Adequate Time Limits): Allowing users enough time to read and complete tasks.
  • Error Prevention and Recovery: Providing clear instructions and easy ways to correct mistakes.

4 Principals of WCAG

Perceivable

Principle 1: Perceivable - Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

This means that users must be able to perceive the information being presented. It addresses the needs of users with visual, auditory, and other sensory disabilities.

Guidelines under Perceivable:
  • Guideline 1.1: Text Alternatives: Provide text alternatives for any non-text content so that it can be changed into other forms people need, such as large print, Braille, speech, symbols or simpler language.
  • Guideline 1.2: Time-based Media: Provide alternatives for time-based media.
  • Guideline 1.3: Adaptable: Create content that can be presented in different ways (for example simpler layout) without losing information or structure.
  • Guideline 1.4: Distinguishable: Make it easier for users to see and hear content including separating foreground from background.

Operable

Principle 2: Operable - User interface components and navigation must be operable.

This means that users must be able to operate the interface. It addresses the needs of users who cannot use a mouse or keyboard, as well as those with time limitations or who experience seizures.

Guidelines under Operable:
  • Guideline 2.1: Keyboard Accessible: Make all functionality available from a keyboard.
  • Guideline 2.2: Enough Time: Provide users enough time to read and use content.
  • Guideline 2.3: Seizures and Physical Reactions: Do not design content in a way that is known to cause seizures or physical reactions.
  • Guideline 2.4: Navigable: Provide ways to help users navigate, find content, and determine where they are.

Understandable

Principle 3: Understandable - Information and the operation of user interface must be understandable.

This means that users must be able to understand both the information and the operation of the user interface. It addresses the needs of users with cognitive disabilities, learning disabilities, and language barriers.

Guidelines under Understandable:
  • Guideline 3.1: Readable: Make text content readable and understandable.
  • Guideline 3.2: Predictable: Make Web pages appear and operate in predictable ways.
  • Guideline 3.3: Input Assistance: Help users avoid and correct mistakes.

Robust

Principle 4: Robust - Content must be robust enough that it can be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

This means that content should be developed using well-structured markup and should conform to accessibility standards to ensure compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies.

Guidelines under Robust:
  • Guideline 4.1: Compatible: Maximize compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies.

WCAG 2.1 and 2.2 Success Criteria

1.1.1: Non-text Content

level: A

description:

All non-text content that serves a purpose—such as images, icons, charts, graphical buttons, and media control symbols—must include a text alternative that conveys the same meaning or function. This ensures accessibility for users who rely on assistive technologies like screen readers. Images that convey information, perform actions, or represent meaningful content must have appropriate alt text. Decorative images that do not convey information should have empty alt attributes (alt="") so they can be ignored by screen readers. Complex images like graphs or charts may need a longer text explanation or reference to nearby text. Avoid using images of text unless necessary, and provide the same content in text format whenever possible. Examples of required alt text: logos, action icons (like play or close), informative photos, and charts. Examples where alt="" is acceptable: borders, spacers, purely decorative icons, or background graphics.

1.3.4: Orientation

level: AA

description:

The content does not restrict its view and operation to a single display orientation, such as portrait or landscape, unless a specific orientation is essential. This means users can view and interact with the content whether their device is held vertically (portrait) or horizontally (landscape), unless it’s absolutely necessary to force one layout (for example, in a piano app or game). This helps users who may have their devices mounted or fixed in one position.

passExamples:

A webpage that works both in portrait and landscape mode on a mobile phone.
A form that adjusts and is readable regardless of screen orientation.
A video player that resizes and repositions its controls based on orientation.

failExamples:

A photo gallery that works only in landscape mode and displays a message asking users to rotate their device.
A mobile app that shows a blank screen in portrait mode and requires the device to be turned.
A website layout that breaks or becomes unusable in one orientation.

1.3.5: Identify Input Purpose

level: AA

description:

"Identify Input Purpose" means that when a website has forms asking for information like name, email, phone number, or address, each input field should clearly indicate its purpose using HTML attributes — especially the autocomplete attribute. For example, using autocomplete="email" tells the browser and assistive technologies that the field is for the user’s email address. This helps in two main ways:

  • Browsers can offer helpful autofill options, saving time for all users.
  • Assistive technologies, like screen readers, can better explain the purpose of each field to users with disabilities.
If the input field is missing the correct autocomplete value, or if the value is wrong, screen reader users may not know what to enter, and the browser may not autofill it correctly. This can lead to confusion and make form completion harder for users with disabilities.

Pass Examples:

  • Input with correct autocomplete for name

  • Input with correct autocomplete for email

  • Input with correct autocomplete for phone number

  • Input with autocomplete for given name (first name)

Fail Examples:

  • Missing autocomplete attribute

  • No clear indication of input purpose

  • Invalid autocomplete value

  • Missing label or description of the field

1.4.3: Contrast (Minimum)

level: AA

description:

This rule makes sure that text is easy to read for everyone, especially for people with low vision. To be readable, the color of the text and the background behind it must be different enough — this is called contrast. The **minimum contrast ratio** depends on the size of the text:

  • Normal text: This means regular body text, usually 14pt (about 18.66px) or smaller. It must have at least a 4.5:1 contrast ratio against the background.
  • Large text: This includes bold text that is 14pt (18.66px) or bigger, or regular text that is 18pt (24px) or larger. It only needs a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio.

If text doesn’t meet these contrast levels, many users — especially those with vision impairments — may not be able to read it clearly. This includes text in buttons, headings, paragraphs, and links.

passExamples:

'<p style="color: #000000; background: #FFFFFF;">Black text on white background</p>'
'<h1 style="color: #003366; background: #EEEEEE;">Dark blue heading on light gray</h1>'

failExamples:

'<p style="color: #AAAAAA; background: #FFFFFF;">Light gray text on white background</p>'
'<span style="color: yellow; background: white;">Yellow text on white background</span>'

1.4.5: Images of Text

Level: AA

Description:

This success criterion requires that text should be displayed using real text (HTML or styled with CSS) rather than images, so that users can zoom in, change font size or color, and use screen readers easily. Images of text should only be used when the text is part of a logo or when it’s essential (for example, a scanned handwritten signature).

Using real text improves accessibility, responsiveness, and overall readability. If you use an image that contains text, users who rely on assistive technologies may not be able to read it unless there's an appropriate text alternative.

Text in images cannot be resized, reflowed, or styled by users—making it harder for people with low vision or reading disabilities.

Pass Examples:

  • Headings, buttons, or labels styled with CSS and real text
  • Text written using HTML tags like <h1>, <p>, or <button>
  • Company logo image that contains text (allowed as an exception)

Fail Examples:

  • Banner image that includes text like “Welcome to Our Site” but does not provide a text alternative
  • Button designed as an image with text inside, rather than using an actual <button> element with real text
  • Menu items shown as images with text instead of regular text with styling

1.4.10: Reflow

Level: AA

Description:

Reflow means that content should adjust properly when users zoom in or use smaller screens—without needing to scroll left and right. Users should only need to scroll up and down to read or use the page.

This helps people with low vision who zoom up to 400%, and mobile users with narrow screens. The content should still be readable and usable, without losing anything or breaking the layout.

There are a few exceptions, like maps, charts, or images where two-way scrolling might be necessary. But for text, buttons, forms, and menus—everything should reflow vertically without requiring horizontal scrolling.

Pass Examples:

  • A website that becomes a single-column layout when zoomed in to 400% on a 1280px screen
  • Menus that turn into a hamburger icon on small screens
  • Text blocks that wrap correctly with no content going off-screen

Fail Examples:

  • Webpages that require side-to-side (horizontal) scrolling to read the text after zooming
  • Text or buttons that get cut off, overlap, or disappear when zoomed in
  • Forms where fields or buttons are moved out of view and require both horizontal and vertical scrolling to use

1.4.11: Non-Text Contrast

level: AA

description:

Visual elements that are important for understanding content or functionality, like buttons, icons, focus indicators, or graph bars, must have a contrast ratio of at least 3:1 against adjacent colors. This helps users with low vision distinguish important components from the background or surrounding elements. It applies to user interface components (like form controls and links) and graphical objects that convey meaning.

passExamples:

A submit button with dark text on a light background, providing a contrast ratio of 4.5:1.
An icon with a 3:1 contrast against the surrounding background.
A visible keyboard focus ring with sufficient contrast on a text input field.

failExamples:

A low-contrast icon (e.g., light gray on white) that is difficult to see.
A button with border and background colors that are too similar (less than 3:1 contrast).
Focus indicators (like outlines) that are nearly invisible due to low contrast.

1.4.12: Text Spacing

level: AA

description:

Imagine you need to read a lot of text online, but it's hard for you because the letters, words, or lines are too close together. This rule says that websites should let you adjust that spacing to make it easier to read. Even if you make the text much more spread out (for example, making the space between lines 1.5 times bigger, or adding more space between words and letters), the website's content should still be clear and everything should still work as it should. This helps people who have trouble reading crowded text, like those with dyslexia, low vision, or certain learning differences.

To achieve this, developers should:

  • Use Relative Units: Whenever possible, use flexible units like `em`, `rem`, or percentages for font sizes, line heights, and spacing (padding/margins). Avoid fixed units like `px` for text and its containers, as this allows the layout to expand and adapt when text spacing increases.
  • Avoid Fixed Layouts: Design your website to be flexible and responsive. Don't set fixed heights on areas that contain text, as this can cause content to be cut off if users increase spacing.
  • Prevent Overlapping or Clipping: Ensure that even with significantly increased text spacing (up to the specified thresholds), text does not overlap with other elements, get cut off, or disappear. All content must remain readable, and interactive elements (like buttons or links) must still be fully usable.
  • Test Your Design: It's important to test your website by simulating these increased spacing settings (e.g., using browser extensions or custom styles) to find and fix any issues where the design might break.

passExamples:

A paragraph remains fully visible and functional when a browser extension increases line height and spacing.
Form labels and inputs still align correctly after increasing letter and word spacing.
No overlapping or cutoff text when users apply custom style sheets with increased spacing.

failExamples:

Text gets cut off or overlaps when line height is increased.
Paragraphs or controls become unreadable when letter or word spacing is adjusted.
Elements break or disappear when custom text spacing styles are applied.

1.4.13: Content on Hover or Focus

level: AA

description:

When extra content appears on hover or focus (like tooltips, dropdowns, or popups), it must be dismissible, hoverable, and persistent. This means users should be able to dismiss it without moving the pointer, keep it visible while interacting with it, and it should not disappear unexpectedly. This helps people with low vision, mobility impairments, or cognitive challenges who need more time to read or interact with extra content.

passExamples:

A tooltip appears when an info icon is focused, and stays visible until focus moves away or the user presses ESC.
Dropdown content stays visible while the user moves the mouse over it.
Popup help text that appears on focus and includes a close button accessible via keyboard.

failExamples:

A tooltip that disappears too quickly when the user tries to move the mouse to it.
Extra content that vanishes as soon as focus is lost, even if the user is trying to read it.
Help text that cannot be dismissed without moving the pointer away.

2.1.1: Keyboard

level: A

description:

All functionality on a website must be usable with a keyboard alone. This helps users who can’t use a mouse, such as screen reader users or those with motor disabilities.

passExamples:

Users can navigate through links and buttons using Tab and Enter.
Menus open and close with arrow keys and Enter key.

failExamples:

Interactive slider that only works with mouse drag.
Buttons that do nothing when focused and Enter is pressed.

2.1.2: No Keyboard Trap

level: A

description:

When using only a keyboard, users should never get stuck on a part of the page. They must be able to move in and out of any interactive element (like a form or dialog) using standard keyboard keys.

passExamples:

A modal dialog that opens and can be closed with the Esc key or Tab key navigation.
An iframe that allows users to enter and exit using Tab or Shift+Tab.

failExamples:

Focus gets stuck inside a video player and doesn’t return to the page.
A popup opens but cannot be closed using only the keyboard.

2.2.1: Timing Adjustable

level: A

description:

If users have a time limit to complete something (like filling a form), they must have options to turn off, adjust, or extend the time unless it’s essential. This is important for users with cognitive disabilities or who need more time.

passExamples:

A session timeout warning appears with a "Stay Signed In" option.
Users can select "Do not timeout" when starting a quiz.

failExamples:

Form times out after 1 minute with no warning.
Online exam that ends automatically with no way to extend time.

2.2.2: Pause, Stop, Hide

level: A

description:

Any moving, blinking, scrolling, or auto-updating content that starts automatically and lasts more than 5 seconds must have a way to pause, stop, or hide it. This avoids distractions and helps users focus, especially those with cognitive or visual disabilities.

passExamples:

An auto-scrolling news ticker with a pause button.
A carousel slider that includes play/pause controls.

failExamples:

A banner that scrolls endlessly with no way to stop it.
Animations that loop and distract users, with no controls.

2.1.4: Character Key Shortcuts

level: A

description:

If a single key shortcut (like just pressing “S”) is used in the content, users must be able to turn it off, change it, or make it work only when a specific area is in focus. This helps people using speech input or screen readers avoid accidental actions.

passExamples:

User can disable or remap the “S” shortcut key in settings.
Shortcut keys only work when the input field or editor is focused.

failExamples:

Pressing “D” deletes a message immediately without warning.
No option to disable keyboard shortcuts for voice control users.

2.3.1: Three Flashes or Below Threshold

level: A

description:

Web content must not flash more than 3 times in one second. Flashing lights can cause seizures, especially in people with photosensitive epilepsy. If flashing is needed, it should stay within a safe limit.

passExamples:

A video with no flashing lights or strobe effects.
Animations that flash only twice per second.

failExamples:

A banner that flashes rapidly 6 times per second.
Game scenes with repeated fast flashing effects.

2.4.1: Bypass Blocks

level: A

description:

Users should be able to skip over repeated blocks of content like navigation menus. This helps keyboard and screen reader users reach the main content quickly.

passExamples:

'<a href="#main-content" class="skip-link">Skip to main content</a>'
'<main id="main-content">Main article starts here</main>'

failExamples:

User must press Tab many times to reach the main content.
No way to skip header and menu links.

2.4.2: Page Titled

level: A

description:

Each web page should have a clear and meaningful title shown in the browser tab. This helps users know what the page is about.

passExamples:

'<title>About Us - Accessible World</title>'
'<title>Login - My Account</title>'

failExamples:

'<title>Untitled Page</title>'
'<title>Home</title> (used for multiple different pages)'

2.4.3: Focus Order

level: A

description:

When navigating using Tab, focus should move in a logical and expected order. This helps users follow the structure and content of the page easily.

passExamples:

Focus moves from menu → search → main content → footer.
Modal opens and focus stays within it until closed.

failExamples:

Focus jumps around the page in a confusing order.
Important buttons are skipped when using the keyboard.

2.4.4: Link Purpose (In Context)

level: A

description:

The purpose of each link should be clear from the link text or the surrounding content. This helps users decide if they want to follow the link.

passExamples:

'<a href="annual-report.pdf">Download Annual Report (PDF)</a>'
'Text: "Visit our FAQ section for more details" with <a href="faq.html">FAQ</a>'

failExamples:

'<a href="info.html">Click here</a>'
'Multiple links labeled "More" with no context'

2.4.5: Multiple Ways

level: AA

description:

Users should be able to find a web page using more than one method, like navigation menus, search, or site maps. This helps users with different needs find information easily.

passExamples:

Main navigation menu + search box + sitemap
List of pages in both menu and footer

failExamples:

Only one navigation path is available to reach the page
No search or site map provided

2.4.6: Headings and Labels

level: AA

description:

Headings and labels must clearly describe the topic or purpose of the content or control. This helps users, especially screen reader users, understand and use the content.

passExamples:

'<label for="email">Email Address</label>'
'<h2>Contact Information</h2>'

failExamples:

'<label>Submit</label> (without saying what is being submitted)'
'<h3>Section 1</h3> (no meaningful title)'

2.4.7: Focus Visible

level: AA

description:

When users navigate using a keyboard (like pressing Tab), the focused element must be visually clear. This helps users know where they are on the page.

passExamples:

Buttons and links show a visible border or color change when focused.
Focus outline appears on input fields.

failExamples:

No visual indicator when an element is focused.
Focus styles removed using CSS (e.g., outline: none)

2.4.11: Focus Not Obscured (Minimum)

level: AA

description:

The element that receives keyboard focus should not be fully hidden behind other content like popups or sticky headers. It should always be at least partially visible.

passExamples:

Focused input field is always visible while scrolling.
Sticky menus don’t cover focused elements.

failExamples:

Sticky header covers the focused link.
Focus moves to an off-screen item with no scroll or visibility.

3.2.1: On Focus

level: A

description:

When a user focuses on an element (like a form field or button), it should not automatically trigger a major change like submitting a form or opening a new page. Changes should only happen when the user chooses to do something, like clicking a button.

passExamples:

'<input type="text" placeholder="Enter name">'
'Focus on a menu does not open a new page until the user clicks.'

failExamples:

Focusing on a dropdown automatically redirects to another page.

3.2.2: On Input

level: A

description:

When users fill in or change something in a form, it should not cause unexpected changes, like submitting the form or refreshing the page, without warning.

passExamples:

Typing in a search box doesn’t submit the search until clicking the button.

failExamples:

Changing a dropdown value automatically submits the form.

3.2.3: Consistent Navigation

level: AA

description:

If a website has the same navigation links on multiple pages, they should appear in the same order and location. This helps users learn and rely on the navigation.

passExamples:

Main menu appears at the top of every page in the same order.

failExamples:

Navigation links are rearranged on different pages.

3.2.4: Consistent Identification

level: AA

description:

Components with the same function should be labeled the same way across pages. This avoids confusion for users.

passExamples:

All search boxes are labeled “Search”.

failExamples:

One search button says “Go” and another says “Find” for the same function.

3.2.6: Consistent Help

level: AAA

description:

If help is available on a page (like live chat, FAQs, or contact info), it should be located in the same place on all pages.

passExamples:

Help button is always on the top-right corner of every page.

failExamples:

Help link appears on the home page only and nowhere else.

3.3.1: Error Identification

level: A

description:

If users make a mistake in a form (like leaving a required field blank), the error must be clearly shown and described so the user knows how to fix it.

passExamples:

“Please enter your email address” appears below a blank email field.

failExamples:

The form reloads with no message, leaving the user confused.

3.3.2: Labels or Instructions

level: A

description:

Form inputs should have clear labels or instructions to help users know what to do.

passExamples:

'<label for="email">Email Address</label>'

failExamples:

Text input with no label or placeholder.

3.3.3: Error Suggestion

level: AA

description:

If an error is found, the system should suggest how to fix it.

passExamples:

“Your password must be at least 8 characters.”

failExamples:

Just shows “Invalid input” with no explanation.

3.3.4: Error Prevention (Legal, Financial, Data)

level: AA

description:

When users submit important information (like for money, data changes, or legal agreements), they should have a chance to review, confirm, or correct it.

passExamples:

A “Review your order” page before final payment.

failExamples:

Clicking “Submit” directly places an order without confirmation.

3.3.7: Redundant Entry

level: AAA

description:

Users should not have to enter the same information more than once in a single process.

passExamples:

Shipping and billing address copied with a checkbox.

failExamples:

Users must enter the same address in two different places with no copy option.

3.3.8: Accessible Authentication

level: AA

description:

Login and authentication processes should be usable without needing to remember or type text like passwords unless there is another easy method like a login link or biometric login.

passExamples:

Allowing sign-in using fingerprint or one-time link.

4.1.2: Name, Role, Value

level: A

description:

This guideline ensures that all user interface elements (such as buttons, checkboxes, sliders, or custom widgets) clearly communicate their name, role, and current value or state to screen readers and other assistive technologies.

For example, if a control is a button, the screen reader should announce it as a button. If it is a checkbox, it should say whether it is checked or not. This helps blind users and keyboard users understand and interact with the controls easily.

If you are creating custom controls using JavaScript or other code, you must use ARIA attributes like role, aria-label, and aria-checked so that assistive technologies can understand them.

passExamples:

A custom checkbox built using a <div> element has role="checkbox", tabindex="0", and updates aria-checked="true" or false when clicked.

failExamples:

A clickable <div> that looks like a button but does not have role="button" or a name, so screen reader users don’t know it’s a button or what it does.

A slider created with JavaScript that moves visually but does not update its value using ARIA, so blind users can’t tell what value they have selected.

4.1.3: Status Messages

level: AA

description:

This guideline helps users with screen readers by ensuring they are informed when something changes on the screen—like an error message, confirmation message, or status update—without needing to move their focus.

For example, if someone submits a form and a message appears saying "Form submitted successfully" or "Email is required", that message should be announced automatically. This is done using ARIA live regions like aria-live="polite" or aria-live="assertive".

This is important for people who cannot see the screen and rely on audio to understand what happened after they performed an action.

passExamples:

A form error message appears in a <div aria-live="polite"> so that screen readers announce the error automatically when it appears.

A confirmation message like "Item added to cart" is placed in a live region, and the screen reader announces it without any user action.

failExamples:

An error message appears visually below the form field but is not inside a live region, so the screen reader user never hears it unless they manually move focus to find it.

A dynamic status message updates on screen (e.g., "Saving...") but is not announced because no ARIA live region is used.

ARIA Attributes

aria-label

The aria-label attribute is used to give an invisible name to an element. It’s helpful when there is no visible text but you want screen readers to speak something meaningful. This is often used for icon buttons, input fields, and elements that need a label but don’t have one. The text in aria-label will only be read by assistive technologies like screen readers.

Example: A search button with a magnifying glass icon.

<button aria-label="Search">🔍</button>

aria-labelledby

The aria-labelledby attribute tells the screen reader to use another element’s text as the label. You must give the label element an id, and use that ID in the aria-labelledby attribute. This is useful when an input or section is described by a heading or label already on the page. It's great for reusing visible text as the accessible label.

Example: Input field labeled by a heading.

<h2 id="name-label">Full Name</h2>
<input type="text" aria-labelledby="name-label">

aria-describedby

The aria-describedby attribute is used when you want to give extra information about an element. Screen readers will first read the label, then read the description from the element referenced by this ID. It’s very useful for form inputs, buttons, or anything that needs additional help text or instructions for users.

Example: Input field with helpful description.

<label for="email">Email</label>
<input type="email" id="email" aria-describedby="email-help">
<small id="email-help">We'll never share your email with anyone.</small>

aria-hidden

The aria-hidden attribute hides elements from screen readers. If you set aria-hidden="true", that part of the page will not be read aloud. This is helpful for decorative elements like icons or repeated visual content that doesn’t add meaning. It improves the screen reader experience by reducing unnecessary information.

Example: Star icon hidden from screen reader.

<span aria-hidden="true">★</span>

role

The role attribute defines what kind of element it is, especially when the element is not a native HTML tag like a button or checkbox. It helps screen readers understand the purpose of the element. Use role to make a <div> act like a button, a <span> act like a checkbox, and so on. It's a powerful tool when creating custom UI components.

Example: A div that behaves like a button.

<div role="button" tabindex="0">Click Me</div>

aria-expanded

The aria-expanded attribute tells whether an element (like a dropdown, accordion, or menu) is currently expanded or collapsed. Screen readers will announce this to help users know if extra content is visible. This is very useful for buttons that show or hide content when clicked.

Example: A button that toggles additional content.

<button aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="details">
  Show Details
</button>
<div id="details" hidden>
  Here are the details!
</div>

aria-checked

The aria-checked attribute is used with custom checkbox elements. It tells screen readers whether the item is selected (true), not selected (false), or mixed. This is useful when building custom toggles or checkbox-like elements that are not standard HTML checkboxes.

Example: A custom checkbox div with selection status.

<div role="checkbox" aria-checked="true" tabindex="0">
  Subscribe to Newsletter
</div>

aria-pressed

The aria-pressed attribute is used on toggle buttons. It lets screen reader users know whether the button is currently in a pressed (active) or unpressed (inactive) state. This is helpful for bold/italic buttons in text editors or like/dislike buttons.

Example: A toggle button for bold text.

<button aria-pressed="false">Bold</button>

aria-live

The aria-live attribute is used for dynamic content updates. It tells screen readers to automatically read changes in a region, even if the user’s focus is somewhere else. This is helpful for alerts, timers, or chat messages. You can use values like "polite", "assertive", or "off".

Example: A polite live region that updates without interrupting.

<div aria-live="polite">
  New message received!
</div>

aria-disabled

The aria-disabled attribute tells assistive technology that an element is disabled, even if it is not visually shown as such. Unlike the HTML disabled attribute, aria-disabled works on more elements and gives developers more control over how things appear vs. behave.

Example: A button that appears clickable but is announced as disabled.

<button aria-disabled="true">Submit</button>

aria-required

The aria-required attribute tells assistive technologies that a form field must be filled out before submitting. It is useful for custom form fields that don’t use the native required HTML attribute. Screen readers will announce that the field is required, helping users understand form rules clearly.

Example: A custom input field marked as required.

<input type="text" aria-required="true" aria-label="First Name">

aria-current

The aria-current attribute indicates the current item in a list, navigation, or set of tabs. It helps screen reader users know which item is currently active or selected. Common values are "page", "step", or "true". It is mostly used in navigation menus to highlight the current page.

Example: Highlighting the current page in a navigation list.

<a href="home.html" aria-current="page">Home</a>

aria-haspopup

The aria-haspopup attribute shows that an element will open a popup like a menu, dialog, or listbox. This helps screen reader users prepare for a new type of interaction. You can also set values like "menu", "listbox", "dialog", or "grid" to describe the popup type.

Example: A button that opens a menu.

<button aria-haspopup="true" aria-controls="menu1">
  Options
</button>

aria-busy

The aria-busy attribute tells assistive technologies that content is loading or being updated. When aria-busy="true", screen readers will wait before reading changes in that region. It is very helpful for dynamic areas like search results or dashboards that load data in real-time.

Example: A content area that is busy loading.

<div aria-busy="true">
  Loading your dashboard...
</div>

aria-level

The aria-level attribute defines the heading level for an element, especially when it's not using a native <h1> to <h6> tag. It tells screen readers the importance or rank of the heading. This is useful when using styled text or <div> elements for headings.

Example: A div used as a level 2 heading.

<div role="heading" aria-level="2">
  Section Title
</div>

aria-selected

The aria-selected attribute indicates if an item in a group (like a tab or a listbox) is currently selected. It helps screen reader users know which item is active among others. This is useful in tab interfaces, selectable lists, or grids where visual selection is shown but needs to be announced accessibly.

Example: One tab is selected among multiple.

<div role="tablist">
  <button role="tab" aria-selected="true">Overview</button>
  <button role="tab" aria-selected="false">Details</button>
</div>

aria-controls

The aria-controls attribute identifies the ID of the element that is being controlled by another element, like a button controlling a panel. It helps users understand which area will change when they interact with the button or control.

Example: A button that shows or hides a section.

<button aria-controls="info-panel">
  Toggle Information
</button>

<div id="info-panel">
  Additional information is shown here.
</div>

role="alert"

The role="alert" makes content automatically announced by screen readers as soon as it appears. This is useful for error messages or important notices that users should know about immediately without moving their focus.

Example: Displaying an error message.

<div role="alert">
  Error: You must fill out all required fields.
</div>

role="dialog"

The role="dialog" is used for modal or popup dialog boxes. It lets screen reader users know they’re inside a dialog and should not interact with the rest of the page until it’s closed. It's often paired with aria-modal="true" and focus trapping.

Example: A modal popup with content.

<div role="dialog" aria-modal="true">
  Are you sure you want to delete this item?
</div>

aria-modal

The aria-modal attribute is used with role="dialog" to tell screen readers that the dialog is modal. This means users should interact only with the dialog content until it's closed. It prevents screen readers from reading other content in the background.

Example: A modal dialog that blocks interaction with the page.

<div role="dialog" aria-modal="true">
  Please confirm your action before continuing.
</div>

aria-hidden

The aria-hidden attribute is used to hide elements from screen readers. When set to true, assistive technologies will ignore the element. This is useful when content is decorative or not meant for accessibility tools, even if it's visible on the screen.

Example: Hiding decorative text from screen readers.

<span aria-hidden="true">★</span>

aria-describedby

The aria-describedby attribute points to the ID of another element that describes the current element. It provides extra information to screen reader users, like helpful instructions or hints.

Example: Adding a hint to an input field.

<input type="text" aria-describedby="hint">
<div id="hint">Enter your full name as shown on ID.</div>

aria-labelledby

The aria-labelledby attribute links an element to another that acts as its label. It tells screen readers to read the content of the referenced label instead of a normal label or aria-label.

Example: Labeling a section with a heading.

<h2 id="profile-heading">User Profile</h2>
<section aria-labelledby="profile-heading">...</section>

aria-label

The aria-label attribute gives a custom text label to an element when there’s no visible label. It helps screen reader users know the purpose of the element, like a button or icon.

Example: Labeling a button with an icon.

<button aria-label="Close">
  <span aria-hidden="true">×</span>
</button>

aria-readonly

The aria-readonly attribute tells assistive technologies that a form control is read-only. The user can read the content but cannot change it. This helps screen reader users understand they cannot edit the field.

Example: A read-only input field.

<input type="text" value="Not Editable" aria-readonly="true">

aria-live

The aria-live attribute is used for regions that update dynamically, like notifications or chat messages. Screen readers will announce any change in that region without needing to focus on it. Values include polite and assertive.

Example: Live announcement area.

<div aria-live="polite">
  You have 1 new message.
</div>

aria-pressed

The aria-pressed attribute is used with toggle buttons to tell whether the button is in a pressed (active) or unpressed (inactive) state. This helps screen readers convey the button's current status.

Example: Toggle button for favorites.

<button aria-pressed="false">
  Add to Favorites
</button>

aria-expanded

The aria-expanded attribute tells screen readers whether a collapsible section or menu is currently expanded or collapsed. When a user toggles content visibility, this value should be updated dynamically.

Example: Toggle FAQ answer section.

<button aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="faq1">
  What is ARIA?
</button>
<div id="faq1">ARIA stands for Accessible Rich Internet Applications...</div>

aria-activedescendant

The aria-activedescendant attribute is used in composite widgets like autocomplete or listbox to show which item is currently active. It helps assistive technologies track the focus without moving the actual input focus.

Example: Keyboard navigation through suggestions.

<input type="text" aria-activedescendant="option2" aria-owns="listbox1">
<ul id="listbox1">
  <li id="option1">Apple</li>
  <li id="option2">Banana</li>
</ul>

aria-valuemin, aria-valuemax, aria-valuenow

These attributes are used in widgets like sliders or progress bars. aria-valuemin and aria-valuemax set the range, while aria-valuenow tells the current value. This allows screen reader users to understand how far along a process is.

Example: A progress bar at 70%.

<div role="progressbar"
     aria-valuemin="0"
     aria-valuemax="100"
     aria-valuenow="70">
  70% Complete
</div>

aria-orientation

The aria-orientation attribute defines whether a widget like a tab list or slider is arranged vertically or horizontally. This helps screen readers announce how the items are displayed so users can navigate accordingly.

Example: Horizontal tab list.

<div role="tablist" aria-orientation="horizontal">
  <div role="tab">Tab 1</div>
  <div role="tab">Tab 2</div>
</div>

aria-multiselectable

Used on a listbox or grid, aria-multiselectable tells screen readers if the user can select more than one item. This is useful in scenarios where users need to select multiple options like in filters or checklists.

Example: Listbox that supports multiple selection.

<ul role="listbox" aria-multiselectable="true">
  <li role="option">Option A</li>
  <li role="option">Option B</li>
</ul>

aria-sort

The aria-sort attribute is used on table headers to indicate the sorting direction: ascending, descending, none, or other. This is very helpful for screen reader users to know how data is sorted.

Example: Column sorted in descending order.

<th aria-sort="descending">Price</th>

aria-dropeffect

aria-dropeffect was used to show what kind of drop action would be allowed in a drag-and-drop interface (e.g., copy, move, link). This attribute is now deprecated, and better alternatives should be used with HTML5 drag and drop.

Example: (Deprecated usage)

<div aria-dropeffect="move">Drop files here</div>

aria-keyshortcuts

The aria-keyshortcuts attribute tells assistive technologies what keyboard shortcut activates the element. It allows users to know that a specific key combo like "Ctrl+S" will perform an action.

Example: Button triggered with Ctrl+S.

<button aria-keyshortcuts="Ctrl+S">Save</button>

aria-keyshortcuts

The aria-keyshortcuts attribute tells assistive technologies what keyboard shortcut activates the element. It allows users to know that a specific key combo like "Ctrl+S" will perform an action.

Example: Button triggered with Ctrl+S.

<button aria-keyshortcuts="Ctrl+S">Save</button>

aria-relevant

aria-relevant defines what types of changes (like additions, text updates, or removals) in a live region should be announced by assistive technologies. It is used with aria-live for better control.

Example: Announce additions in a live chat.

<div aria-live="polite" aria-relevant="additions"></div>

aria-flowto

The aria-flowto attribute specifies the next element in the reading flow. It’s used when the visual order differs from the logical DOM order, helping screen readers follow the correct reading sequence.

Example: Directing screen readers to jump to another section.

<div id="box1" aria-flowto="box2">Step 1</div>
<div id="box2">Step 2</div>

aria-current

The aria-current attribute tells screen readers which item is currently active, such as in a menu, breadcrumb, or pagination. It improves orientation for visually impaired users.

Example: Highlighting the current page in a menu.

<li aria-current="page">Home</li>

aria-current

The aria-current attribute tells screen readers which item is currently active, such as in a menu, breadcrumb, or pagination. It improves orientation for visually impaired users.

Example: Highlighting the current page in a menu.

<li aria-current="page">Home</li>

aria-autocomplete

Used in input fields, aria-autocomplete tells screen readers if autocomplete suggestions are available. It can have values like inline, list, or both, helping users understand how they can choose suggestions.

Example: Autocomplete for a search bar.

<input aria-autocomplete="list" aria-owns="suggestions">
<ul id="suggestions">
  <li>Apple</li>
  <li>Banana</li>
</ul>

aria-colspan

aria-colspan defines how many columns a cell should span in a grid. It helps screen readers understand the structure of complex grids, just like the HTML colspan attribute in tables.

Example: A cell spanning two columns.

<div role="gridcell" aria-colspan="2">Merged Column</div>

aria-rowspan

The aria-rowspan attribute defines how many rows a cell spans in a grid or table structure. It works similarly to the HTML rowspan and helps screen readers interpret table layout correctly.

Example: A cell spanning two rows.

<div role="gridcell" aria-rowspan="2">Merged Row</div>

aria-colindex

aria-colindex tells assistive technologies the column number for a grid or table cell. This is helpful when table headers are complex and don’t appear in the usual DOM order.

Example: A cell at column 3.

<div role="gridcell" aria-colindex="3">Data</div>

aria-rowindex

The aria-rowindex attribute tells screen readers the row number of a cell in a table or grid. It is useful for creating accessible data grids with proper navigation for screen reader users.

Example: A cell in row 2.

<div role="gridcell" aria-rowindex="2">Item</div>

aria-placeholder

aria-placeholder provides placeholder text for assistive technologies when the native HTML placeholder isn't enough or is visually hidden. It describes the expected input.

Example: Input for entering a city name.

<input type="text" aria-placeholder="Enter your city">

aria-hidden

The aria-hidden attribute hides content from screen readers. It's useful when certain visual elements (like decorative icons) should not be announced.

Example: Icon hidden from screen reader.

<span class="icon" aria-hidden="true">★</span>

aria-labelledby

Use aria-labelledby when an element's label is provided by another element on the page. The attribute references the ID of the labeling element to make the relationship clear to screen readers.

Example: Labeling a form section.

<h2 id="formTitle">User Details</h2>
<form aria-labelledby="formTitle">...</form>

aria-controls

aria-controls identifies the element that is controlled by the current element. For example, a button might control the visibility of a panel or tab content.

Example: Button controlling a panel.

<button aria-controls="infoPanel">Toggle Info</button>
<div id="infoPanel">Additional Information</div>

aria-posinset

The aria-posinset attribute defines an element’s position in a set. It helps users understand their current position among siblings (like "3 of 5").

Example: Item 2 of 4 in a list.

<li role="option" aria-posinset="2" aria-setsize="4">Option 2</li>

aria-setsize

aria-setsize tells assistive technologies the total number of items in a set or list. When used with aria-posinset, it helps screen readers provide context like “item 2 of 5”.

Example: Set of 4 options.

<li role="option" aria-posinset="1" aria-setsize="4">Option 1</li>

aria-valuemin, aria-valuemax, aria-valuenow

These attributes are used for elements with roles like slider or progressbar. They define the minimum, maximum, and current values, which screen readers use to announce progress or position.

Example: A slider at value 70.

<div role="slider" aria-valuemin="0" aria-valuemax="100" aria-valuenow="70"></div>

aria-flowto

aria-flowto tells screen readers what element should be read next, especially when the order is not the same as the visual layout. This helps users follow content more easily.

Example: After reading paragraph1, go to paragraph2.


<p id="paragraph1" aria-flowto="paragraph2">
  Step 1: Turn on the switch.
</p>
<p id="paragraph2">
  Step 2: Wait for the light.
</p>

aria-haspopup

This attribute tells screen readers that clicking or focusing on an element will open a popup, like a menu or dialog. It improves awareness of interactive elements.

Example: A button opens a menu.


<button aria-haspopup="true" aria-controls="menu1">
  Open Menu
</button>
<div id="menu1" role="menu">...</div>

aria-multiselectable

Use aria-multiselectable on listboxes or grids to let screen readers know multiple items can be selected. It's helpful for checklists or selection tools.

Example: A list where multiple items can be selected.


<ul role="listbox" aria-multiselectable="true">
  <li role="option">Item 1</li>
  <li role="option">Item 2</li>
</ul>

aria-braillelabel

This attribute gives a label specifically for braille displays. It may differ from the spoken label and helps provide better accessibility for braille readers.

Example: Label for a braille display.


<button aria-label="Submit form" aria-braillelabel="Braille: Submit">
  Send
</button>

aria-brailleroledescription

This attribute customizes the role name spoken or shown on a braille display. It is useful when the default role name is too long or not meaningful for braille users.

Example: Shortened braille role for a switch.


<div role="switch" aria-brailleroledescription="Sw">On/Off</div>

aria-keyshortcuts

Use aria-keyshortcuts to inform users of available keyboard shortcuts. This makes your app more user-friendly for keyboard-only users.

Example: A button with a shortcut key.


<button aria-keyshortcuts="Ctrl+S">Save</button>

aria-errormessage

aria-errormessage provides a reference to an error message element that explains what's wrong with the input. This improves form validation messages for screen readers.

Example: Input with an error message.


<input id="email" aria-invalid="true" aria-errormessage="error1">
<div id="error1">Please enter a valid email address.</div>

aria-roledescription

This gives a custom role description that screen readers announce. It replaces the default role name with something more meaningful or familiar to users.

Example: Role description for a custom slider.


<div role="slider" aria-roledescription="volume control"></div>

aria-details

The aria-details attribute links to another element that provides more detailed information. It’s useful for charts, summaries, and tooltips.

Example: Element with additional explanation.


<div aria-details="info1">COVID Data</div>
<div id="info1">This data shows infection rates from Jan to March.</div>

aria-required

aria-required indicates that a field is required to complete a form. Screen readers will notify users so they don’t miss important inputs.

Example: Required input field.


<input type="text" aria-required="true">
